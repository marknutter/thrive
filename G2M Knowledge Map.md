## 2. POSITIONING: THE PARALLAX STORY

### 2-Minute Elevator Pitch (Core Narrative)

> "Most teams think they have a resource problem. In practice, it's a predictability problem that shows up in pricing, profit, hiring, and delivery."

**The Hook:** You're planning the future with tools designed to track the past.
- Delivery tools show what's happening today
- Finance systems explain what already happened
- Neither was built to plan what will happen next

**That's where Parallax fits:** Connects delivery, real capacity, and future demand into one forward-looking view.

## 3. GTM NARRATIVE (Full Framework — "What Resonates" Structure)

### Opening: You Don't Have a Resource Problem
*"You do not have a resource problem. You have a profit and predictability problem."*

**Money symptoms:**
- Revenue swings feast/famine — no visibility into capacity vs upcoming work
- Margin targets missed even when everyone looks busy on paper
- Forecasts start solid, then drift when projects slip or large deal lands
- Pricing/hiring/contractor decisions made "with a knot in your stomach"

**People symptoms:**
- Teams bounce between slow weeks and meltdown weeks
- Senior talent reshuffles plans instead of doing expert work
- Holidays/resignations create chaos — no forward view of who's needed where
- Burnout and turnover creep up

**Client/growth symptoms:**
- Win work before confident the team can deliver it profitably
- Delay or turn away good opportunities because staffing feels too risky
- Clients feel the inconsistency → renewals and expansions harder
- Can't answer: "What will revenue, utilisation, and hiring look like 90 days from now?"

### The Real Problem Behind the Symptoms
The issue: Leadership can't clearly answer:
1. Can we hit revenue/margin goals with current + pipeline work?
2. When do we genuinely need to hire vs are already overstaffed?
3. Which clients/project types are mispriced, quietly destroying profit, burning out people?

### Why Current Tools Can't Fix This

**Delivery tools (Monday, ClickUp, Asana, Jira, Smartsheet):**
- ✅ Organize tasks, make project status visible, day-to-day execution
- ❌ Only show committed work — not backlog or pipeline
- ❌ Don't connect staffing/timelines to revenue, pricing, margin, billable capacity
- ❌ Frame as delivery problem not forecasting/profit problem

**All-in-one ERP (Deltek, NetSuite, Workamajig):**
- ✅ Financial/payroll source of truth, billing, invoicing, financial reporting
- ❌ Forecasts anchored in accounting, not flexible future scenarios
- ❌ Scenario planning is slow/manual → decisions go back to spreadsheets

**The gap:** Delivery tools show how work gets done. Finance shows how money is counted. Neither connects people, projects, pipeline, pricing, and profit.

*"That gap is exactly where Parallax lives."*

### Status Quo Beliefs to Break (Challenger Method)

**Belief 1:** "Forecasting is fine in spreadsheets or PM tools."
- Reality: Manual updates, backward-looking data, assumptions disconnected from real capacity, no integration to demand or financial signals

**Belief 2:** "Resource management is task assignment."
- Reality: Task assignment answers who does the work TODAY. Strategic resource planning answers whether the business has the right capacity to achieve FUTURE goals.

**Belief 3:** "More detailed project plans create more accurate forecasts."
- Reality: Granularity increases noise, not accuracy. Project health metrics don't forecast margin, workload balance, future availability, delivery risk, or staffing needs.

### Category Definition: Strategic Resource and Capacity Planning
Parallax is NOT:
- A project management system
- A PSA or accounting platform
- A scheduling tool

Parallax IS:
- The strategic planning and forecasting layer connecting delivery, real capacity, and future demand
- A system for profit and predictability, not task scheduling
- An early warning system for pricing and staffing risk

## 4. PERSONA GUIDE (Extracted Frameworks)

### Segment Buying Dynamics
- **SMB (15–50):** Founder-led. Fast, centralised, practical decisions.
- **Mid-market (50–150):** C-level led, broad stakeholders. Alignment matters as much as pain.
- **Enterprise (150+):** VP/Director-led evaluation, C-level approval. CEO involvement is rare.

### CEO / Founder Persona
**Who they are:** Often started as creatives, strategists, consultants. Still understand utilisation, blended rates, staffing mix.

**Core problems:**
- Forecasts drift as projects slip and pipeline timing changes
- Resourcing pressure hidden by average utilisation
- Hiring decisions feel uncomfortable without forward view
- Leaders spend time reconciling different versions of the future

**Beliefs to challenge:**
- "Our experience fills the gaps."
- "More detail will make forecasts reliable."
- "Resourcing is an operational problem, not a strategic constraint."

**Buying triggers:**
- Growth step-change, new practice launch, major client win
- Repeated forecast surprises
- Resourcing becoming a leadership problem
- Board/partner scrutiny on predictability

**Discovery questions:**
- "Which decisions feel hardest to make with confidence today?"
- "Where does resourcing become a problem first — specific roles, teams, or practices?"
- "When plans change mid-quarter, what usually caused it?"
- "What work do you hesitate to take on because staffing feels risky?"

**Sample messaging:**
- *"Most firms can see today clearly. The risk sits in resourcing decisions made before you can see three months ahead."*
- *"Once people are assigned, you have largely locked margin and delivery options. The job is to spot pressure before that happens."*

### CFO / Finance Leader Persona
**Who they are:** Models people, time, and uncertainty. Revenue depends on delivery effort, staffing mix, utilisation, timing.

**Core problems:**
- When resourcing assumptions are wrong, margin moves before finance can see it in reports
- Needs future-facing numbers believable enough to act on

**Deal role:** Validator in all segments. Co-buyer in mid-market. Approver/risk gate in enterprise.

## 6. OUTBOUND CAMPAIGN PLANNING TEMPLATE (Framework)

### Core Philosophy
- Good outbound personalization driven by the LIST as much as the message
- Tight targeting = relevant messaging doesn't need to be complex
- Goal of outreach: NOT to sell the product. To sell the idea that a conversation will create value
- Quality over volume: lists under 500 accounts, ideally ~300; AEs use 25–100 account range
- Max 2 roles/individuals per company at same time
- Cadences: NOT email-only — always include LinkedIn touches + phone/voicemail

### AI Usage for Outreach (Jeff's actual instructions)
1. Complete the campaign template → paste into ChatGPT → ask for cadences/messaging
2. Experiment with prompts: consultative, challenger, direct — get multiple options
3. DON'T copy AI-generated messages word for word
4. Remove AI artefacts: em dashes, overly polished phrasing, generic filler
5. "Introducing the occasional subtle punctuation, grammar, or spelling imperfection is acceptable if it helps the message feel genuinely human."

### Campaign Planning Template Structure
1. **Campaign Goal:** One clear outcome. Meetings/Pipeline/Re-engagement. How success measured. Timeframe.
2. **Target Customer:** ICP (industry, size, geography). Signals/context/trigger. Clear disqualifiers.
3. **Target Persona:** Job title/seniority. Core responsibility. Primary pain.
4. **Data needed:** For personalization.
5. **Problem-Value Hypothesis:** Situation → Problem → Impact → Outcome (single hypothesis)
6. **Angle to Lead With:** Pain-led / Insight-led / Trigger-led / Proof-led (pick one primary, one secondary)
7. **Offer:** Type of conversation/asset. What they get. Time commitment.

### Outreach Prompt Template (Jeff's exact prompt for AI-generated sequences)
```
Context:
- Highly targeted campaign, small well-defined account list
- Recipient is specific persona with clear buying context or trigger
- Objective: secure conversation by demonstrating relevance and value
- Messaging aligned to [company's] core themes
- Avoid product-led or feature-heavy language
- Do not use em dashes or overly polished phrasing

Task:
Create a 3-touch outbound email cadence.
Each email should introduce a distinct angle and avoid repeating earlier messages.
Messages should feel human, direct, and commercially credible.

Guidelines:
Email 1: Standalone email. Open with buying context/trigger + clear problem–value hypothesis.
Email 2: In-line reply to Email 1. Bump/nudge format that adds new insight or reframing.
Email 3: Short, direct follow-up. Low pressure, clear ask, minimal context.

Output: Subject + body for Email 1. Emails 2+3 as in-line replies.
Keep each under 100 words.
Provide two tone variations per email (e.g., consultative + direct).
Output only the emails. No explanations or commentary.
```

## 7. SALES PROCESS (Stage Model)

### Core Philosophy
*"Pipeline stages reflect buyer decisions, not sales activity."*
*"Speed differs. Standards do not."*

### Stage Model
0. **Target / Prospect** — Exit: meeting scheduled (discovery call or intro demo)
1. **Discovery Accepted** — Buyer willing to discuss how they plan the business today
2. **Accepted Need for Change** ← FIRST HARD GATE — Buyer accepts current approach creates risk
3. **Solution Agreed** — Buyer agrees they need a dedicated planning system
4. **Decision Process Confirmed** — Real deal with defined decision path
5. **Commit / Close**

### Stage 1: Discovery — Topics That Must Be Covered
- What prompted the conversation
- What they're trying to plan or forecast
- How they do that today
- Where confidence breaks down

### Stage 2: Accepted Need for Change — Key Points to Make
- PM tools show committed work, NOT future capacity
- Finance systems explain outcomes, NOT upcoming risk
- Spreadsheets break as assumptions change
- People, work, revenue, and margin are NOT connected in one forward view

*"No selling in this stage."*

Exit requirements (NON-NEGOTIABLE):
- Buyer EXPLICITLY states they are guessing, reacting late, or lacking confidence
- Buyer AGREES continuing as-is is not acceptable
- Interest in the product does NOT move the deal forward

### Stage 4: Decision Process Options (MM/Enterprise only)
- **Paid Pilot (preferred):** Buyer sees value, no clear path to full contract. Time-bound, paid, attached to standard contract with defined exit point
- **Free POC (last resort):** Only for fundamental technical/data unknowns (integrations, data structure, feasibility)
- *Never offer both. Sales must align internally first.*

*"No date means no deal."*

## 8. DEMO PHILOSOPHY: 11 NO-NOS (Callum's Framework)

### Core Insight from Callum
*"A demo isn't an SC-owned event or a standalone deliverable. It's a team-delivered solution session, shaped by discovery, aligned to the deal strategy, and designed to move a specific decision forward."*

**Key principles:**
1. The demo is NOT where you start — it's the validation of the story already anchored earlier in the cycle
2. Just because a prospect asks for a demo doesn't mean they're ready for one
3. "Saying yes too early can mean we give up control rather than helping the buyer build alignment and momentum"
4. Liking the product ≠ liking us ≠ being aligned internally or ready to move
5. A quiet room doesn't always mean conviction
6. Clear intent. Fewer things shown. Stronger links between what we show and WHY it matters for that specific customer

**Why demos go wrong:**
- Deals stall after demos
- Processes drag
- Discounting to recreate momentum
- Struggle to build champions who can sell internally
- These are signs the problem, value, or decision wasn't anchored early enough

**Callum's direction:**
*"Be more deliberate together. Clear intent. Fewer things shown. Stronger links between what we show and why it matters for that specific customer. This helps the customer as much as it does us."*

Build content engine that produces strategic content → cuts into campaign-ready assets tied to monthly themes → distributes across ABM + broader marketing channels.

Asset types:
- Short-form video clips
- Quote graphics
- LinkedIn posts (company + individual contributor)
- Blog posts/summaries
- Newsletter content
- Email sequences for outbound
- Ad creative from top-performing assets

**Every asset traces back to a content pillar + a campaign theme.**

### Distribution Channels
- **Channel 1: ABM** — Cut assets delivered to sales team. Podcast/content for relationship-building with strategic prospects. Customer stories matched to prospect verticals.
- **Channel 2: Marketing** — Association/partner promotion, social media, LinkedIn, newsletter.

### Sales Feedback Loop
*Content improves when it reflects what's happening in real sales conversations.*
Objections, questions, and themes from prospect calls → inform podcast topics, blog posts, web event themes.

## 12. CALLUM'S GTM ENABLEMENT — 4-DECK CURRICULUM (Extracted)

### Deck 1: Stage 0 — Building Pipeline (Outbound)

**What Outbound IS:**
- Narrative-led motion that introduces POV
- Crafted to create interest with right buyers
- Value-oriented, not product-oriented
- Scaled deliberately to priority segments
- Aligned to ICP and personas

**What Outbound is NOT:**
- Indiscriminate outreach without a POV
- Product explanation or feature messaging
- Activity optimized solely for raw volume
- A substitute for later-stage discovery

*"Outbound earns attention through relevance and perspective."*

**The Compounding Effect of Outbound (4 phases):**
1. Find quick win deals → Create conversations → Enter sales process
2. Progress active opportunities
3. Build familiarity and credibility / Shape how market understands problem
4. Create future inbound and reactivation / Warm accounts before timing is right

*"The best outbound compounds over time."*

**Outbound as a System (3 elements — all must be strong):**
- Targeting → Messaging → Cadence → Conversation → Channel
- "When one element is weak, volume increases but outcomes decline."

**Roles in the Outbound Motion:**
- **GTM/Marketing:** Narrative distribution at scale, market education, early signals
- **BDRs:** Coverage and consistency, scaled execution, converting signals into conversations
- **AEs:** Precision and relevance, strategic account access, POV-led engagement
- **Leadership:** Executive-level relationship access, credibility reinforcement, selective/high-leverage engagement

**LinkedIn vs Email:**
- LinkedIn: Visibility-based, relationship/credibility-led, softer CTAs, builds credibility over time, long-term pipeline
- Email: Direct, permission-based, clear value exchange, more explicit CTA, shorter feedback loop, near-term conversations
- *"Same intent. Different execution. Both necessary."*

**AI in Outbound — Callum's Rules:**
- "AI is an execution accelerator, not a thinking replacement."
- AI generates options, not answers
- Humans own strategy and judgment
- Output must be edited and humanized
- **NEVER COPY AND PASTE**
- Planning precedes AI automation

### Deck 2: Demo Strategy

**What a Demo IS:**
- A tool to progress a qualified deal
- Used at a specific stage
- Designed to achieve a specific outcome

**What a Demo is NOT:**
- A default next step
- A platform tour
- A way to "keep momentum"

*"If a session doesn't meet this definition, it should not be booked as a demo."*

**Entry Bar — Must Answer Before Booking:**
1. What stage are we in?
2. What buyer decision are we supporting?
3. What risk or uncertainty are we reducing?
4. What should be different after this session?

*"If we can't answer these, don't run the demo yet"*

**Demo Types by Stage:**
- **Stage 1-2:** Intro/Diagnostic Demo → Pressure-test the problem
- **Stage 3:** Solution Demo/Deep Dive → De-risk solution and adoption
- **Stage 4:** (if needed) → De-risk the decision
- **Stage 5:** → De-risk the commitment

**Intro/Diagnostic Demo (Stages 1-2):**
- Short, discovery-led walkthrough
- Focused on planning questions, not features
- Success = buyer describes planning question(s) they can't answer, gaps are visible
- Must NOT be: feature tour or justification to move deal forward

**Solution Demo (Stage 3):**
- Scenario-based, uses buyer language
- Shows how you solve their specific planning gap
- NOT generic product tours or open-ended exploration

**3-Part Value-Based Agenda:**
- Challenge → Opportunity → Validation
- Each section: Frame the value → Show the relevant product moment → Pause and confirm understanding
- *"Tell. Show. Tell. Repeat x3."*

**How to Measure Demo Success:**
- ✅ Buyer understanding shifted
- ✅ Intended outcome achieved
- ✅ Deal progressed to desired next state
- ✅ Risk reduced or removed
- ❌ No objections ≠ success
- ❌ Technical wins only matter if the deal moves

### Deck 3: Sales Process Overview

**Why They Changed from MEDDIC:**
- "MEDDIC was setting us up for failure by forcing qualification of un-educated buyers"
- Compensated with heavy reliance on product and services to close
- Deals became transactional and deliverable-led

**New Process Principles:**
- Stages reflect buyer decisions, not deliverables
- Speed may differ by company/segment; consistency does not
- Interest alone ≠ progression

**What Makes a Real Deal?**
- Within ICP
- Their current approach does not give confidence in future planning
- They recognize the need for a more formal planning system

**6-Stage Process:**
1. **Intro + Discovery** — Establish agreement on the problem(s) we solve
2. **Problem Aligned** — Align value as the solve to agreed challenges
3. **Solution Validation** — Verify product can support their requirements
4. **Deal Alignment** — Align stakeholders around product, value, decision
5. **Commercial Alignment** — Resolve commercials and risk to get commitment
6. **Closed Won** — Contract signed

**What the Buyer Is Deciding at Each Stage:**
1. "Is this problem worth discussing?"
2. "Does this deliver more value than our current approach?"
3. "Will this product work for us?"
4. "What do we need to get to a decision?"
5. "Can I commit and sign this?"

**Stage Regression Rules:**
- New product questions → move back to Solution Validation
- Decision ownership/timing unclear → move back to Deal Alignment
- Commercial risk resurfaces → remain in Commercial Alignment

**Demo Rules (built into the process):**
- Demos support decisions; they do not move stages on their own
- Discovery and Problem Alignment = diagnostic demos only
- Solution Validation = full solution demos and deep dives

**Closing mantra:** *"CONTROL. INTENT. DISCIPLINE."*

### Deck 4: Deal Management and Strategy

**Market Reality:**
- Buyers operating in uncertainty, internal pressure, risk avoidance
- Decisions take longer, more stakeholders, default to "do nothing"
- *"Uncertainty → Risk Aversion → Indecision"*
- *"If we don't actively reduce risk and guide decisions, deals stall by default."*

**How Deals Move Forward:**
- Risk is actively reduced for buyer
- Progress feels safer than waiting
- Decision process is led by someone trusted

**The Core Shift: Activity-Led → Strategy-Led:**

| Activity-Led | Strategy-Led |
|---|---|
| Responding to requests | Decisions are guided |
| Demos create momentum | Risk is surfaced early |
| Decisions emerge late | Momentum is intentional |

*"If an action doesn't guide a decision, it's activity — not strategy."*

**The Intentional Deal Cycle:**
1. Make plan: What risk are we reducing? What decision are we moving? What commitment do we want?
2. Run play (discovery, demo, email, proposal)
3. Check impact: Did risk reduce? Did the buyer move?
4. Adjust: Progress → Change approach → Pause/reset

*"No action without an expected impact"*

**3 Things to Strategize For:**
- **Risk:** What would make the buyer hesitate or stop?
- **Momentum:** How do we know something's moved?
- **Decisions:** What decision is being made now? By who?

**Before Every Action:**
1. What risk does this reduce?
2. What decision does this move?
3. What commitment do we expect?

**After Every Action:**
1. Did risk reduce?
2. Did a decision move?
3. Did we get the commitment?

**Strategic Operating Rhythm (4 tools):**
1. **Deal Reviews** — Focus on next decision, not status updates. No commitment = no progression.
2. **Deal Strategy Documents** — Required for all deals. Clarify how we win and what could block us.
3. **Call Reviews** — Did the call reduce risk or move a decision? Did we secure the expected commitment?
4. **Mutual Action Plans (MAPs)** — MAPs reflect decisions already agreed to. Check-ins are decision alignment calls. Collaboration over persuasion.

**Strategy by Stage:**
- Stage 1-2: Discovery framing, not demos → Pressure-test the problem
- Stage 3: Focused demo + validation → De-risk solution and adoption
- Stage 4: Stakeholder mapping, MAP → De-risk the decision
- Stage 5: Commercial clarity, exec call → De-risk the commitment
